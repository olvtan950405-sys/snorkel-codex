# Walkeeper recovery contract

All input files are UTF-8. TSV files have a header, no duplicate records, and decimal integers without separators. WAL arithmetic uses PostgreSQL's fixed segment size from `policy.json`; a WAL name is 24 uppercase hex digits: 8 for timeline, 8 for logical log, and 8 for segment. Convert the last two fields to a byte position as `(log * 2^32) + (segment * segment_size)`. Segment numbers that would begin at or beyond `2^32` bytes within a log are invalid.

`backups.tsv` has `backup_id`, `started_at`, `timeline`, `start_lsn`, `start_wal`, and `target_lsn`. Times are canonical UTC instants ending in `Z`; LSNs are uppercase `HEX/HEX`. A backup begins at `start_lsn` in `start_wal` and seeks recovery through `target_lsn` on its named timeline. `segments.tsv` has `name`, `timeline`, `start_lsn`, `end_lsn`, `size`, `sha256`, and `state`. State is `complete`, `partial`, or `corrupt`; only `complete` is usable. Its size must equal the policy segment size, its filename timeline and start position must agree with its fields, `end_lsn - start_lsn` must equal the segment size, and `sha256` must be exactly 64 lowercase hex characters.

`history.tsv` has `timeline`, `parent`, and `switch_lsn`. Timeline 1 has no row. Every other timeline used by a backup or segment must have exactly one history row, parent timelines must be smaller, and following parents must terminate at timeline 1 without a cycle. A child contains its parent's history strictly before `switch_lsn`; at and after the switch it requires child-timeline WAL. Thus recovery from a backup follows the unique timeline path to its target: for each interval, require every segment intersecting `[start_lsn,target_lsn)`, switching to the next child exactly at its switch LSN. A switch LSN must be segment-aligned. Extra segments and segments from sibling forks never satisfy a gap.

A backup verdict uses the first applicable reason in this order: `invalid_backup`, `unknown_timeline`, `invalid_history`, `start_segment_mismatch`, `target_before_start`, `wal_gap`, `invalid_segment`, then `ok`. `required_segments` contains the ordered names of usable segments for an `ok` backup and is empty for a rejection. A target exactly on a segment boundary does not require the following segment.

`policy.json` has `segment_size`, `minimum_recoverable`, `keep_newest`, and `protected_backup_ids`. Select the recoverable backup with greatest `target_lsn`, then greatest `started_at`, then lexicographically smallest ID. Retention keeps the selected backup, every protected backup, and the newest `keep_newest` recoverable backups by `(started_at, backup_id)` descending. It then adds older recoverable backups until at least `minimum_recoverable` are kept. A protected but unrecoverable backup is kept, but contributes nothing to that minimum.

Keep every segment required by every kept recoverable backup. Also keep any complete, structurally valid segment whose start is at or after the greatest target LSN among kept recoverable backups; these are future recovery material. Delete all other segment records, including partial, corrupt, malformed, sibling-fork, and obsolete complete segments. Lists are lexicographically sorted except each backup's `required_segments`, which is recovery order.

The output is compact canonical JSON (keys sorted recursively, no insignificant whitespace, one final newline). Its exact shape is `{"backups":[{"backup_id":string,"reason":string,"recoverable":bool,"required_segments":[string]}],"retention":{"delete_backups":[string],"delete_segments":[string],"keep_backups":[string],"keep_segments":[string]},"schema_version":1,"selected_backup":string|null}`. Backup entries are sorted by ID. Unknown columns are ignored; missing required columns or malformed `policy.json` is an error and must not produce a partial report.
